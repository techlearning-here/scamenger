# Scam experience stories — structure

This document defines the **structure for scam experience stories** on the Stories page. Use it as an editorial template when writing or commissioning stories, and as a reference for data/CMS schema when storing full story content.

---

## 1. Purpose

- **Stories** are first-person or anonymized accounts of real scam experiences.
- They help readers **recognize red flags**, **relate to victims**, and **learn what to do** (report, recover, protect themselves).
- A consistent structure keeps stories scannable, **emotionally clear**, and useful for SEO and learning.
- **Emotional connection** is central: when readers feel the situation (hope, doubt, shame, relief), they remember the story and are more likely to spot similar traps and take action.

---

## 2. Emotional aspect and connecting readers

Stories should **show the emotional journey** and **put the reader in the situation** so they can think: *“That could be me.”*

### 2.1 Why emotion matters

- **Recognition:** Readers in a similar situation (e.g. new romance, job hunt, money stress) see themselves in the victim’s hopes and doubts.
- **Memory:** Emotion makes the story stick—readers recall “that story where they felt so stupid” when they face a similar offer or request.
- **Validation:** Victims who read the story feel less alone; their feelings (shame, anger, grief) are named and normalized.
- **Action:** Feeling the stakes (loss, fear, relief) makes “report” and “protect yourself” feel relevant, not abstract.

### 2.2 How to show emotion and connect readers

| Principle | What to do |
|-----------|------------|
| **Name feelings** | Use clear emotion words: *hopeful*, *suspicious*, *ashamed*, *relieved*, *angry*, *stupid*, *afraid to tell anyone*. Avoid vague “I felt bad.” |
| **Relatable hooks** | In Character intro and Initial plot, include details many share: *lonely*, *needed extra money*, *wanted to believe*, *didn’t want to seem rude*, *under pressure at work*. |
| **Inner voice** | In Victim’s experience, use the victim’s self-talk: *“I told myself…”*, *“Part of me wondered…”*, *“I didn’t want to admit…”*. This builds “I would have thought that too.” |
| **Specific moments** | One or two concrete moments (e.g. *“The moment I clicked Send on the transfer”*, *“When his number disappeared”*) anchor the reader in the situation. |
| **Sensory and situation** | Short, concrete details (time of day, where they were, what they were doing) make the scene real without identifying anyone. |
| **Validate, don’t lecture** | Acknowledge that smart people get scammed; avoid “I should have known.” Frame learning as “what I know now” rather than “I was dumb.” |
| **Hope as well as pain** | Where true, include recovery or small wins (reporting, telling someone, learning)—so the story doesn’t end only in despair. |

### 2.3 Sections that carry the emotional weight

- **Character intro** — Reader thinks: *“That’s like me.”* (life situation, what they wanted)
- **Initial plot** — Reader thinks: *“That sounds normal.”* (why it didn’t feel like a scam)
- **Victim’s experience** — Reader thinks: *“I’d have believed that too.”* (hopes, doubts, self-justification)
- **Victim’s pain** — Reader feels: *“That’s real; I’m not alone.”* (shame, loss, fear named clearly)
- **Climax** — Reader feels the turn: *“That’s the moment everything changed.”*
- **Learning (victim)** — Reader hears a peer: *“They get it; they’re not judging.”*

Use these as a checklist when editing: each section should add to the reader’s emotional connection to the situation.

## 3. Story sections (in order)

| # | Section | Purpose | Notes |
|---|---------|---------|--------|
| 1 | **Character intro** | Who the victim is (anonymized): context that helps readers relate (e.g. age range, life situation, what they were hoping for). | 1–3 sentences. No real names or identifying details. |
| 2 | **Initial plot** | Setting the scene: how the victim came into contact with the scam (platform, offer, person, or situation). | What seemed normal or appealing at first. |
| 3 | **Scam experience** | What actually happened: the mechanics of the scam—requests, promises, pressure, fake sites, fake people. | Factual; helps readers recognize the *pattern*. |
| 4 | **Victim’s experience** | The victim’s perspective at the time: what they thought, felt, and believed as it unfolded. | Builds empathy and shows how easy it is to be fooled. |
| 5 | **Victim’s pain** | Emotional and practical impact: shame, fear, loss of money or identity, relationship damage, stress. | Honest but not sensational. Validates readers who have been through similar pain. |
| 6 | **Climax** | The moment things broke: realization, confrontation, or point of no return (e.g. money gone, account locked, “person” disappeared). | Clear turning point in the narrative. |
| 7 | **Learning (victim)** | What the victim learned from their own experience: red flags they now see, what they would do differently. | Personal takeaways. |
| 8 | **Learning for readers** | Actionable takeaways for the reader: how to spot this scam, what to do if it happens, where to report, how to protect themselves. | Bullets or short paragraphs; link to guides and report flow where relevant. |

## 4. Section definitions (copy-paste template)

Use the headings and prompts below when writing or editing a story.

### 4.1 Character intro

**Purpose:** Brief, anonymized context so readers can relate. **Emotional goal:** Reader thinks *“That’s like me.”*

**Prompt:** Who is this person (without identifying them)? E.g. life stage, what they were looking for (love, job, investment, help), or what made them vulnerable. Include one or two **relatable emotional hooks** (e.g. lonely, stressed about money, wanted to trust, didn’t want to seem paranoid).

**Length:** 1–3 sentences.

**Example tone:** *“I was in my 40s, recently divorced, and had started dating again online. I wasn’t looking for get-rich-quick—just someone to talk to.”*

---

### 4.2 Initial plot

**Purpose:** Set the scene—how the victim first encountered the scam. **Emotional goal:** Reader thinks *“That sounds normal.”*

**Prompt:** Where did it start? (App, website, call, message.) What was offered or who approached them? What seemed normal or appealing? Include a **concrete detail** (e.g. time of day, where they were) or **why it felt safe** so the reader can picture the situation.

**Length:** 2–5 sentences.

**Example tone:** *“He messaged me on a dating app. His profile looked real, and we moved to WhatsApp. He was friendly, asked about my day, and after a few weeks mentioned he’d made money trading—and could show me how.”*

---

### 4.3 Scam experience

**Purpose:** Describe what actually happened—the mechanics of the scam. (Emotion lives more in Victim’s experience and Pain; here keep one or two **emotional beats** if they fit, e.g. “I was relieved when the first withdrawal worked.”)

**Prompt:** What did the scammer do? (Requests for money, fake platforms, fake emergencies, fake identity, pressure tactics.) What did the victim do in response? Optionally include a **single moment** the reader can feel (e.g. clicking Send, seeing the “account locked” message).

**Length:** 1–2 short paragraphs.

**Example tone:** *“He sent a link to a ‘trading platform.’ I put in a small amount; it showed gains. When I tried to withdraw, they asked for ‘tax’ and ‘fees.’ I paid. Then my account was locked and he stopped replying.”*

---

### 4.4 Victim’s experience

**Purpose:** The victim’s inner experience—what they thought and felt at the time. **Emotional goal:** Reader thinks *“I’d have believed that too.”*

**Prompt:** What did the victim believe? Why did they trust? What doubts did they have (if any) and why did they ignore them? Use **inner voice** and **named feelings**: e.g. *“I told myself…”*, *“Part of me wondered…”*, *hopeful*, *embarrassed*, *didn’t want to seem paranoid*. This section is the main bridge between reader and situation.

**Length:** 1 short paragraph.

**Example tone:** *“I thought we had something real. When he asked for money, I told myself it was temporary—that we were building a future. I was embarrassed to ask anyone else because I didn’t want to hear that it was a scam.”*

---

### 4.5 Victim’s pain

**Purpose:** Acknowledge the emotional and practical impact. **Emotional goal:** Reader feels *“That’s real; I’m not alone.”* Validates victims and helps others grasp the cost.

**Prompt:** What did the victim lose or suffer? **Name emotions clearly**: shame, fear, anger, grief, isolation, feeling stupid, loss of trust. Include both **practical** (money, time) and **emotional** (couldn’t tell anyone, stopped doing X). Avoid sensationalizing; be honest and specific so readers in the same situation feel seen.

**Length:** 1 short paragraph.

**Example tone:** *“I lost over $12,000. Worse was the shame—I couldn’t tell my family. I stopped dating for a long time and still find it hard to trust. It took months to report because I felt so stupid.”*

---

### 4.6 Climax

**Purpose:** The turning point—realization or point of no return. **Emotional goal:** Reader feels the moment everything changed.

**Prompt:** When did the victim realize it was a scam, or when did things finally break (money gone, person gone, account locked)? Use **one concrete moment** (e.g. “His number was gone,” “The screen said account locked”) and a short line of **felt reaction** (e.g. “That’s when I knew,” “My stomach dropped”) so the reader is in the moment.

**Length:** 2–5 sentences.

**Example tone:** *“One morning his number was gone. The app said ‘user not found.’ I Googled the platform name and found warnings. That’s when I knew. I’d been talking to strangers the whole time.”*

---

### 4.7 Learning (victim)

**Purpose:** What the victim learned from their own experience. **Emotional goal:** Reader hears a peer—*“They get it; they’re not judging.”*

**Prompt:** What would they do differently? What red flags do they see now? What do they want others to know? Use **“what I know now”** rather than **“I was stupid”** so victims feel validated and readers feel encouraged, not blamed.

**Length:** 1 short paragraph or 3–5 bullets.

**Example tone:** *“I now know: if someone I’ve never met asks for money, it’s a scam. Real relationships don’t depend on crypto or ‘investments.’ I wish I’d checked the platform name and talked to someone I trust before sending a single dollar.”*

---

### 4.8 Learning for readers

**Purpose:** Actionable takeaways for anyone reading.

**Prompt:** How can readers spot this scam? What should they do if it happens to them? Where can they report or get help?

**Length:** Bullet list or short paragraphs; link to relevant guides (e.g. romance scam guide, report flow, recovery resources).

**Example tone:**  
- *Watch for: love interest who quickly moves to another app and later mentions investing or money.*  
- *Never send money or share financial details with someone you haven’t met in person.*  
- *If you’ve lost money: report to [FTC / local authority] and our [Report a scam] page.*  
- *Read our [Romance scam guide] for more red flags and recovery steps.*

## 5. Optional fields (metadata)

For each story, keep consistent metadata (already in use where applicable):

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Slug** | URL-safe identifier (e.g. `how-i-lost-12000-someone-i-never-met`). |
| **Title** | Short, impactful headline (used on cards and story page). |
| **Category** | Scam category (e.g. financial, impersonation, online) for filtering and related stories. |
| **Hardship tag** | Optional: major_loss, emotional, recovery, identity, moderate. |
| **Reading time** | Optional: e.g. “~4 min read” (derived from word count when content exists). |

## 6. Data / CMS mapping (for implementation)

When you add full story content (static data, CMS, or API), each section can map to a field or block:

| Section | Suggested field name | Type |
|---------|----------------------|------|
| Character intro | `characterIntro` or `intro` | Text (1–3 sentences) |
| Initial plot | `initialPlot` or `setup` | Text |
| Scam experience | `scamExperience` or `whatHappened` | Text (longer) |
| Victim’s experience | `victimExperience` or `myPerspective` | Text |
| Victim’s pain | `victimPain` or `impact` | Text |
| Climax | `climax` or `turningPoint` | Text |
| Learning (victim) | `victimLearning` or `myTakeaways` | Text or list |
| Learning for readers | `readerLearning` or `takeawaysForReaders` | Text or list (can include links) |

Stories can be stored as one long narrative (single `body` or `content` field) with clear headings for each section, or as separate fields for structured display (e.g. accordions, “Key takeaway” callouts).

## 7. Suggested changes to the structure

Use this list to refine the template, data model, and story page UX.

### 7.1 Narrative order

| Change | Suggestion | Reason |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **Pain vs climax order** | Keep default **Victim’s pain** (5) → **Climax** (6). Optionally allow **Climax** → **Pain** per story when the “moment I realized” is the main beat. | Most stories: pain then climax. Some read better as: “Then I realized it was a scam” (climax) → “Afterward I felt…” (pain). Document both in the template. |
| **Merge Victim’s experience + Victim’s pain** | Optional: combine **Victim’s experience** and **Victim’s pain** into one section (“What I felt during and after”) for shorter stories. | Reduces repetition; some stories don’t need two separate “how I felt” blocks. Keep as two sections for longer stories. |

### 7.2 New or optional sections

| Section | Purpose | When to use |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Recovery / aftermath** | What the victim did after (reporting, support, steps taken). | When the story includes reporting, recovery, or “where I am now.” Fits after Climax and before Learnings. |
| **Red flags (list)** | Bullet list of concrete red flags in *this* story. | Always useful for scanning; can be part of “Learning for readers” or a separate callout. |
| **Content warning** | Short line at the top (e.g. “Contains description of financial loss and emotional impact”). | For stories with major loss, identity theft, or trauma. Optional field in metadata. |
| **Disclaimer** | One line: “This is one person’s experience. We don’t verify every detail.” | Protects the site; add once on the story template or in footer of each story. |

### 7.3 Metadata and data model (code alignment)

| Change | Suggestion | Reason |
|--------|------------|--------|
| **Excerpt** | Add optional **excerpt** (1–2 sentences) to `ScamStoryEntry` or story content. | Used on story cards and in SEO description when full body exists. |
| **Reading time** | Compute from word count when body/sections exist; store or derive “~N min read.” | FeatureList 17t; improves cards and SEO. |
| **Structured vs single body** | Support both: (a) **single body** (Markdown/HTML with headings) and (b) **structured sections** (separate fields). | Single body is easier for writers; structured sections enable “Key takeaway” callouts and section-specific styling. Prefer single body + consistent H2/H3 for the 8 sections so one source works for both. |
| **Link to guide** | Add optional **relatedGuideSlug** (e.g. `romance`, `phishing`) to link “Learning for readers” to the matching scam guide. | Drives traffic to `/us/scams/[slug]/` and reinforces learning. |

### 7.4 Editorial and safety

| Change | Suggestion | Reason |
|--------|------------|--------|
| **Anonymization** | Add explicit rules: no real names, locations (or only country/region), employer names, or contact details. | Protects victims and avoids defamation. |
| **Tone** | Add guideline: “Respectful and factual; avoid sensationalizing or blaming the victim.” | Keeps stories helpful and avoids re-traumatizing. |
| **Length** | Suggest soft limits: e.g. 400–800 words for “short” and 800–1,500 for “long” so stories stay scannable. | Helps writers and keeps reading time predictable. |

### 7.5 Story page UX (when content exists)

| Change | Suggestion | Reason |
|--------|------------|--------|
| **Key takeaway callout** | Render “Learning for readers” (and optionally “Learning (victim)”) in a highlighted box at the top or after the story. | Surfaces actionable content for skimmers. |
| **Jump links** | Optional in-page links to sections (e.g. “Jump to: Red flags · What happened · Learning”). | Helps long stories and accessibility. |
| **Related guide CTA** | Under “Learning for readers,” add a CTA: “Read our [Romance scam] guide” (from category or `relatedGuideSlug`). | Connects stories to guides and report flow. |

### 7.6 Summary of suggested changes

- **Order:** Keep Pain → Climax → Learnings; document alternative Climax → Pain where it fits the story.
- **New optional sections:** Recovery/aftermath, Red flags list, Content warning, Disclaimer.
- **Data:** Add excerpt, reading time (derived or stored), optional relatedGuideSlug; support single body with consistent headings (and optionally structured fields later).
- **Editorial:** Add anonymization rules, tone guideline, and soft word-count ranges.
- **UX:** Key takeaway callout, optional jump links, related guide CTA when full content is implemented.

---

## 8. Summary

- **Eight sections** in order: Character intro → Initial plot → Scam experience → Victim’s experience → Victim’s pain → Climax → Learning (victim) → Learning for readers.
- Use this as the **editorial template** for every scam experience story.
- When implementing full story content, map these sections to your data model or CMS and render them with consistent headings and optional “Key takeaway” or “Learning for readers” callouts on the story page.
- See **§2** for **emotional aspect and connecting readers**; **§7** for suggested changes for optional sections (recovery, red flags, content warning), metadata (excerpt, reading time, related guide), editorial rules, and story-page UX ideas.
